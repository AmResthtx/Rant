The Ground Is Shifting Near Bammel North Houston — Here's What That Means for Your Foundation

When the ground opens up in one place, it rarely stops there. If you own property nearby, your foundation deserves a second look.

This isn't just a road problem.

Sinkholes don't happen in isolation. They're a sign that the soil underneath is moving — and in Houston's clay-heavy ground, when one area starts to shift, nearby foundations can be next. The sinkhole at Bammel North Houston and Veteran Memorial is a visible symptom of what's happening underground.

You may not see anything wrong with your foundation today. But Houston clay soil expands in wet weather and contracts in drought — and that cycle puts constant pressure on whatever is holding your property up. If it's concrete, that pressure adds up over time.

Recent Work
We recently completed a helical pier installation on a residential property in Huffman, Texas — right next to Lake Houston. That's some of the most challenging soil in the region. The installation held. The homeowner didn't have to come back.

Why helical piers — and why not concrete?

Helical piers are screwed down through the unstable clay layer into the load-bearing soil below it. They don't rely on surface conditions. That means when Houston dries out and the clay shrinks — which it will — the pier doesn't move with it.

Concrete piers are less expensive up front. But they sit in the same clay that's causing the problem. Given enough drought cycles, they shift too. Then you're doing the repair twice.

On Cost
